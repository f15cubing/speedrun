# Sub-prompt 09 — Transfer evidence + mechanisms: does olympiad training transfer to GRE-style performance?

You are a specialist research agent in the **science of learning, transfer of training, and quantitative education research**. This is the **empirical crux** of the project's olympiad hypothesis (spec Dimensions 2 and 3). The question is open and must be tested, not assumed: **Does training on olympiad-style problems improve performance on a timed, breadth-heavy, multiple-choice undergraduate math exam like the GRE Mathematics Subject Test — and if so, through what mechanisms?** Your default posture is skeptical: the burden of proof is on demonstrating *far* transfer.

## Exact question and scope

IN SCOPE — answer both:

1. **Transfer evidence.**
   - Summarize the **transfer-of-training literature** relevant here: near vs. far transfer; Thorndike's identical elements; Barnett & Ceci's (2002) taxonomy of transfer; Detterman's skeptical "transfer is rare" position; Perkins & Salomon (low-road/high-road). Establish the prior probability of far transfer in general.
   - Find and evaluate **any direct empirical evidence** on competition/olympiad math training affecting performance on standardized or curriculum-based math tests (e.g., studies of math-circle or competition participants, AMC/AIME → SAT/AP/placement outcomes, problem-solving interventions and standardized scores). For each study: design (RCT? quasi? correlational?), sample, outcome measure, effect, and confounds.
   - **Selection-confound scrutiny:** explicitly address that strong students self-select into olympiad training, so raw correlations between competition experience and exam scores are not causal. Identify whether any study controls for prior ability.

2. **Mechanisms (only if any benefit is plausible).** For each proposed channel, rate evidence strength and label empirical vs. theoretical:
   - **Fluency / automaticity** of algebraic and computational moves.
   - **Comfort with novelty / hard problems** reducing freeze-up on unfamiliar GRE items.
   - **Test anxiety / stress inoculation** from high-pressure competition.
   - **Faster pattern recognition / heuristic repertoire** (Polya/Schoenfeld heuristics).
   - **Deeper conceptual schemas** that make standard problems trivial.
   - For each: is there evidence it *actually* moves a timed-MC-exam score, or is it a plausible story? Be explicit.

Coordinate with sub-prompt 04 (general learning-science effect sizes) — here, focus specifically on **olympiad/competition-math → standardized-exam transfer**, not generic study techniques.

## Deliverable format and length
- ~1300–1900 words.
- Section A: transfer-theory prior (what the literature says about how often far transfer happens) with named positions and where they disagree.
- Section B: direct-evidence table (study / design / sample / outcome / effect / confound-control / verdict). If direct studies are scarce, say so explicitly and rate the evidence base as thin.
- Section C: mechanisms table (channel / empirical-or-theoretical / evidence strength / does it plausibly move a timed-MC score?).
- Section D: a blunt causal-claim verdict — is "olympiad practice raises GRE Subject performance" **supported / plausible-but-unproven / unsupported**, and what study would actually settle it.

## Sourcing requirements
- Prefer peer-reviewed transfer research and meta-analyses; name the researchers (Barnett & Ceci, Detterman, Perkins & Salomon, Thorndike, Schoenfeld, etc.).
- Empirical claims about competition-math outcomes must cite the study; correlational findings must be labeled correlational.
- Per claim: source, type, confidence rating. Separate **[empirical]** from **[theoretical/plausible]** on every mechanism.
- Never invent an effect size or a study. If you cannot find direct evidence, the honest finding "no direct evidence located; reasoning is by analogy to transfer theory" is acceptable and expected.

## Counter-evidence / weak-spot demands
- Lead with the **skeptic's case**: transfer research broadly finds far transfer is rare and hard to elicit; selection effects explain most olympiad↔exam correlations; time spent on olympiad problems is time not spent on GRE-format practice (the identical-elements view predicts format-specific practice wins).
- Flag publication bias and the gap between lab transfer studies and real exam-score outcomes.
- Distinguish "olympiad training helps strong students who'd score well anyway" from "olympiad training causes score gains for a typical GRE Math taker."

## Source list (REQUIRED, at the very bottom)
End your deliverable with a `## Sources` section listing **every** source you explicitly cited **and** any source you drew ideas from, each with author(s)/title/publisher/year and a working link or DOI where available, grouped by type (peer-reviewed / institutional / named expert / practitioner). No uncited claims.

End with a self-confidence summary and a one-line verdict on whether the transfer claim is empirically supported, plus the single biggest evidence gap.
