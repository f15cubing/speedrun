# Critique 09 — Transfer evidence + mechanisms

**Verdict: excellent, accept — this is the epistemic centerpiece of the olympiad thread.** It did exactly what a skeptical evidence review should: established a low prior for far transfer, searched for direct evidence, found essentially none, characterized the one weak positive datapoint honestly, and refused to overclaim. Verdict **PLAUSIBLE-BUT-UNPROVEN** is the correct, defensible answer.

## What is genuinely useful (carry forward) — [strong]
- **No direct evidence exists** that olympiad training raises GRE Subject (or any standardized timed-MC math) scores. The honest "the evidence base is THIN" headline is the single most important finding for the user's pet hypothesis.
- **The one positive datapoint is weak and correctly caveated:** Rebholz/Golle/Trautwein 2022 (N=201 German *grade 3–4*, quasi-experimental, training group started **~1 SD higher** = the selection confound made visible; standardized-test transfer significant in **grade 4 only**). Not GRE format, not GRE population, not randomized.
- **Selection dominates** observed olympiad↔score correlations; the burden of proof is on the causal claim. [strong]
- **Mechanism honesty:** the channel with the strongest *mechanism-level* empirical backing is **anxiety/stress-inoculation** (Saunders et al. 1996), and even that is indirect (competition ≠ formal SIT). The "content" channels (schemas, heuristics, fluency) are **near transfer in disguise** — they only help where olympiad work overlaps GRE content. This dissolves the illusion of "far-transfer dividend."

## Problems / calibration notes
1. **The brain-training analogy may be slightly too pessimistic.** Owen et al. 2010 / Sala & Gobet concern transfer across *largely non-overlapping* domains (n-back → fluid IQ). Olympiad→GRE shares actual mathematical content (number theory, combinatorics, algebra), so it is **less far** than the brain-training case. The response does acknowledge content-overlap near transfer, so the net verdict is right — but in synthesis, state it precisely: *far* transfer of general problem-solving is unsupported; *near* transfer on overlapping content is plausible and is the only defensible channel. Don't let the brain-training analogy imply "zero."
2. **Rebholz effect size ("~0.45 SD") is doing a lot of rhetorical work** for one quasi-experiment in 9-year-olds; keep it tagged [moderate, single weak study] and never generalize it to adult GRE takers.
3. **The proposed settling RCT is exactly our ablation in miniature** (olympiad vs. format-matched practice, equal time, baseline-matched, real-exam outcome) — but prompt 11 shows we can't power it in a week. So we can *design* it and run an underpowered/descriptive version; we cannot *settle* it. Connect these in synthesis.

## Decision
- **No follow-up.** SQ9 closed; conclusions are stable and unlikely to shift with another round.
- **Carry to synthesis [strong]:** "olympiad practice raises GRE Subject performance" is **not empirically supported as a causal claim**; defensible benefit is limited to near-transfer content overlap + anxiety reduction, and likely helps already-strong students more than the median taker. This is the "say it plainly when the premise isn't supported" moment.
