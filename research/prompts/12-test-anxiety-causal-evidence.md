# Sub-prompt 12 — Does test anxiety *cause* underperformance, and does reducing it raise scores?

You are a specialist research agent in **educational/clinical psychology and psychometrics**. A study app for the **GRE Mathematics Subject Test** (a timed, ~66-item, 5-option multiple-choice, breadth-heavy quantitative exam) is considering a "test-anxiety inoculation" feature. Before designing anything, we must test the foundational premise, NOT assume it. Your default posture is skeptical.

## The exact question (answer all four)

1. **Magnitude of the test-anxiety ↔ performance relationship.** Report the best meta-analytic estimates (pooled correlation/effect size **with CIs** and key moderators):
   - General/academic test anxiety: Hembree (1988, *Review of Educational Research*); Seipp (1991); von der Embse, Jester, Roy & Post (2018, *Journal of Affective Disorders*).
   - **Math-specific** anxiety (distinguish this from general test anxiety): Ashcraft & Kirk; Hembree (1990); Foley et al. (2017); **Barroso et al. (2021, *Psychological Bulletin*) meta-analysis** of math anxiety–achievement.
   - State vs trait anxiety; the cognitive (worry) vs affective (emotionality) components (Liebert & Morris); the Cognitive Test Anxiety Scale (Cassady & Johnson 2002).

2. **Causal models — interference vs deficit.** Lay out the two competing accounts and weigh the evidence:
   - **Interference/"choking"**: anxiety consumes working memory / attentional control *at test time*, so a prepared student underperforms.
   - **Deficit**: poor study skills / low mastery cause *both* anxiety and low scores (anxiety is a marker, not a cause).
   State explicitly what the evidence says about which dominates, and for whom (e.g., does interference appear mainly in students who actually know the material?).

3. **The confound (this is the crux).** To what extent is the anxiety–score correlation explained by **prior ability, preparation, or working-memory capacity**? Identify designs that try to break the confound: experimental manipulations of anxiety holding preparation constant; cross-lagged/longitudinal; within-person designs. Be explicit about how much of the raw correlation survives controls.

4. **Do interventions move OBJECTIVE scores?** The decisive question. Separate two outcomes everywhere: **(a) reduces self-reported anxiety** vs **(b) improves objective test performance**. What do Hembree (1988) and later reviews/RCTs find for behavioral/cognitive/skills interventions on *actual scores* (effect sizes + CIs)? Where interventions reduce anxiety but not scores, say so.

Scope IN: academic/standardized testing, ideally college-age and quantitative/math, timed high-stakes exams (closest analog to the GRE Subject). Scope OUT: treatment of clinical anxiety disorders generally; sports/music performance anxiety (admissible only as explicitly-labeled analogy).

## Deliverable format and length
- ~1100–1700 words.
- Section A: effect-size table (construct / population / pooled r or d + CI / source / confidence).
- Section B: interference vs deficit — evidence and verdict.
- Section C: the confound — how much survives controls; best causal designs.
- Section D: intervention evidence split into self-reported-anxiety vs objective-score columns.
- Section E: a blunt verdict — *is reducing test anxiety a credible causal lever on GRE-type scores, and for whom?*

## Sourcing requirements
- Prefer peer-reviewed meta-analyses and primary studies; name the researchers (Hembree, Zeidner, von der Embse, Cassady, Ashcraft, Beilock, Eysenck, Barroso) and note disagreements.
- Per claim: source, type, confidence rating; **always separate [self-report outcome] from [objective-score outcome]** and **[correlational] from [causal/experimental]**.
- Never invent effect sizes. If the causal evidence is thin, say so plainly.

## Counter-evidence / weak-spot demands
- Lead with the **deficit-model / confound** case: how much of the effect is just under-preparation or low WM capacity wearing an "anxiety" label.
- Address **publication bias / small-study effects** in the test-anxiety-intervention literature, and **demand/placebo effects** that inflate self-reported (but not objective) gains.
- Flag generalization limits to **adult, high-stakes, timed quantitative** exams specifically.

## Source list (REQUIRED, at the very bottom)
End with a `## Sources` section listing **every** source you explicitly cited **and** any you drew ideas from, with author(s)/title/venue/year and a working link or DOI where available, grouped by type (peer-reviewed / institutional / named expert / practitioner). No uncited claims.

End with a self-confidence summary and a one-line verdict on whether test anxiety is a causal, manipulable contributor to timed-math underperformance (and the single biggest threat to that claim).
