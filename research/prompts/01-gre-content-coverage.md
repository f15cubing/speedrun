# Sub-prompt 01 — GRE Mathematics Subject Test: authoritative content map, format, and scoring norms

You are a specialist research agent. Produce a precise, citable reference on the **GRE Mathematics Subject Test (current online format, since Sept 2023)**. This will be used to (a) build a topic coverage map for a study app and (b) understand how raw performance becomes a 200–990 scaled score. Do not pad. Distinguish facts you can cite from inferences.

## Exact question and scope

IN SCOPE:
1. The **official ETS content outline** — every topic and sub-topic ETS lists, with the stated approximate percentage weighting (calculus ~50%, algebra ~25%, "additional topics" ~25%). Reproduce the outline at the finest granularity ETS publishes (e.g., under Calculus: single/multivariable, sequences/series, differential equations, etc.; under Algebra: linear algebra, abstract algebra, number theory; under Additional: real analysis, topology, complex variables, probability/statistics, numerical analysis, discrete/combinatorics, etc.).
2. **Exam format**: number of questions (66), options per question (5), total time (2h50m), single section vs. multiple, equal weighting of questions, any guessing penalty (confirm rights-only scoring), online delivery details since Sept 2023.
3. **Scoring mechanics**: how the raw score (number correct, confirm no wrong-answer penalty currently) is converted to the **200–990 scaled score**. State explicitly whether ETS publishes a fixed raw→scaled conversion table or whether each form is **equated separately** (and what that implies for predicting a scaled score).
4. **Norms / percentiles**: the most recent ETS interpretive data — percentile ranks for scaled scores, mean/SD if published. Confirm or correct the project's working assumption that **~57–58/66 correct ≈ 90th percentile ≈ scaled ~880**.

OUT OF SCOPE: test-prep strategy advice, commercial prep-course marketing claims, the general GRE (revised General Test) — only the **Math Subject Test**.

## Deliverable format and length

- ~800–1400 words.
- Section A: content outline as a nested list, each leaf tagged with ETS's weighting bucket.
- Section B: format facts table.
- Section C: scoring + equating explanation (this is the most important part for downstream score modeling — be precise about whether a stable raw→scaled mapping exists publicly).
- Section D: percentile/norm table with the source year.
- Section E: a short "implications for predicting a scaled score" note — flag honestly if no public, stable raw→scaled function exists.

## Sourcing requirements

- **Primary source must be ETS** (official GRE Math Subject Test pages, the official practice book/PDF, the interpretive/score-data tables). Cite the specific document and year for each fact.
- Secondary corroboration (AMS, university math-department advising pages) allowed only to corroborate, clearly labeled.
- For EACH nontrivial claim, give: the source, its type (primary ETS / institutional / practitioner), and a confidence rating, e.g. `[high — ETS official practice book 2023]` or `[low — single university advising page]`.
- If a fact (e.g., current percentile tables, post-2023 conversion) is **not publicly available**, say so plainly and mark it unverified. Do not invent percentile numbers or a conversion table.

## Counter-evidence / weak-spot demands

- Explicitly address: does ETS publish a single raw→scaled table, or is each administration equated? If equated, state that any app projecting a 200–990 number is approximating an unpublished function and explain the consequence.
- Flag any place where commonly-cited "57–58/66 = 880" figures trace back to old paper-format data or forum lore rather than current ETS documents.
- Note any changes between the pre-2023 paper format and the current online format that affect content weighting, timing, or scoring.

End with a self-confidence summary: which Sections are solidly sourced vs. which rely on stale or non-primary sources.
